# 🃏 Deckbuilding Principles

> **Goal:** Build the smallest deck that can beat the next important fights.
> A card is worth adding only if it improves the deck more than keeping the deck smaller would.

---

## Main Rule

**Draft for the next test, not for an imaginary final deck.**

Ask what the deck currently lacks:

- Frontload damage
- Block
- Draw / consistency
- Scaling
- AOE
- Energy support
- Exhaust / cleanup

Take cards that solve a **real weakness** in upcoming fights. Skip cards that are merely acceptable.

---

## Act-Based Drafting

### Act 1
The starter deck is weak — be more willing to add cards, but not automatically. Take cards that materially improve:

- Elite fights
- The known Act 1 boss matchup
- Current damage / block / draw problems

Skip mediocre cards that do not solve a real problem.

### Act 2+
Be much more selective. The deck should have a direction by now.

- Do not add low-impact filler.
- Consistency matters more than collecting "pretty good" cards.

---

## When to Skip

Skip when:

- All options are low impact
- The cards are off-plan
- They solve a problem you do not have
- They require support you do not currently have
- Adding them would bloat a deck that is already functioning

**Skipping is good when deck quality is more valuable than deck quantity.**

---

## Removal Priority

Remove in this order:

1. Curses
2. The worst permanent card in the current deck plan
3. Usually basic Strikes early
4. Basic Defends only when damage density is sufficient and block cards are stronger

**Do not apply these rules blindly.** Context matters:

- Keep Strikes longer in Strike-synergy decks
- Be cautious removing Defends before the deck has real block
- Remove whichever basic card contributes **least** to the current plan

Early shop removal is often strong, but compare it against high-impact purchases.

---

## What Makes a Card Good

A card is good when it does at least one of these:

- Solves a current weakness
- Improves consistency through draw, exhaust, or card selection
- Provides efficient block or damage for its cost
- Gives scaling the deck can realistically support
- Has strong synergy with cards or relics already in play
- Helps in the next elite or boss fight

**Cards that do two useful things for one card draw are especially strong.**

---

## What Makes a Card Bad

- Low impact without fixing a real weakness
- Needs support the deck does not have
- Conflicts with the current direction
- Too slow for upcoming fights
- Worsens the energy curve without enough payoff
- Adds junk or variance the deck cannot absorb

Do not add cards just because they look better than Strike or Defend in isolation.

---

## Powers & Scaling

Scaling is important, but **only when the deck can afford it.** A scaling card is justified when:

- Upcoming fights will last long enough
- The deck can survive the setup turn
- The deck still has enough frontload and block to reach that scaling

Fast practical improvement is often better than slow theoretical power.

---

## Synergy Discipline

Prefer synergy that **already exists** over synergy that might exist later. Take support after you have the payoff, or payoff after you have the support. Do not speculate on packages that aren't real yet.

| Synergy Type | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Strike synergy | Enough Strikes to justify it |
| Exhaust synergy | Actual exhaust volume or exhaust rewards |
| Strength payoffs | Strength generation already present |
| Block payoffs | Real block generation already in place |

---

## Boss & Matchup Awareness

Always evaluate card rewards against the known upcoming boss and likely elites.

| Boss | Prioritize |
|---|---|
| **Guardian** | Reliable block, consistency, controlled damage |
| **Slime Boss** | Frontload, split control, useful AOE |
| **Hexaghost** | Consistent output and survivability |

**Do not draft in a vacuum.**

---

## Pivoting

- Do not force a preconceived archetype.
- Commit only when the deck has real evidence for that direction.
- If the game offers a stronger package than your current plan, **pivot**.

But do not pivot every floor. A coherent medium-strength plan is better than five half-built synergies.
